Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics describe and summarize the characteristics of a dataset or sample data. By using descriptive statistics, we can summarize large amounts of observed data in a meaningful way. Measure the spread of data

Measures of spread quantify how data values deviate from the central tendency, like the mean. In other words, Spread of the observations or data measures the variation of the data around the central tendency such as the mean.

The The three common measures of the spread of the data are, the range, the variance and the standard deviation

1. The Range

The range of the data is defined as the difference between the maximum and minimum values:

Range = maxValue - minValue

The range value between the age of Arsenal's first team, including the coach is: 41 - 22 = 19

Considerations:
  • The Rnage is a single value and not an interval.
  • The Range can be come very large if the min or max value is an extreme value, therefore it is not a good idea to report the range as the only measure of the spread of data.

The Interquartile Range

The IQR (pronounced ‚Äúeye-cue-are‚Äù) represents the spread of the middle 50% of values in a dataset. The IQR focuses on the central portion of the data, ignoring extreme values.

Quartiles divide a dataset into four equal parts, providing valuable insights into data distribution.

  1. First Quartile (Q1), also known as the lower quartile, Q1 represents the 25th percentile. It‚Äôs the value below which 25% of the data falls.
  2. Second Quartile (Q2), it divides the dataset into two equal halves. It‚Äôs the middle value‚Äîhalf the data falls below it, and half above it.
  3. Third Quartile (Q3), also called the upper quartile, Q3 is the 75th percentile. It‚Äôs the value below which 75% of the data lies.

The difference between the quartiles is called the interquartile range (IQR). It captures the spread of the central 50% of data, ignoring extreme values.

IQR = Q3 - Q1
